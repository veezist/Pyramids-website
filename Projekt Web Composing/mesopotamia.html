Mesopotamia


Mesopotamia, which is located between the two rivers: Euphrates and Tigris, around 2000 BC began to create pyramidal structures called ziggurats. However, there were not many of them because they were built of bricks dried by the sun. Colors, on which ziggurats were painted, are golden / brown. It was believed that these buildings were flats for the gods, and therefore they were not the place of public services and ceremonies. Every city in Mesopotamia had its own God patron, who could be close to humanity through the ziggurat. Only priests could enter the center of the ziggurat and their duties included taking care of the needs of the gods. The result was that the priests were very powerful members of the Sumerian society. There are 32 known ziggurats in mesopotamias. Most of them are in Iraq and 4 are in Iran.



Known ziggurats:
Chqa Zanbil - in spite of the eight-year Iran-Iraq war in the 80s, in which many archaeological sites were destroyed, this ziggurat survived and makes it one of the best-preserved ziggurats.

The Sialk- He comes from the beginning of the third millennium BC in Kashan, Iran and is the oldest known zigradate.

Finally, the best known ziggurat:
Tower of Babel - mentioned in the bible book of Genesis. The temple tower was erected at the center of the world, as the axis of the universe. Here, a straight line connected earth and heaven.
